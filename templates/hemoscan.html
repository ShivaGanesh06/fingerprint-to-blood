Blood Type Science

ABO System

The ABO blood group system classifies blood into four main types (A, B, AB, O) based on the presence or absence of antigens on red blood cells.

Rh Factor

The Rh factor (positive or negative) is another important antigen that affects blood compatibility, especially important during pregnancy.

Clinical Importance

Correct blood typing is crucial for safe transfusions, organ transplants, and managing pregnancy complications.

Blood Type Characteristics

Type A
Contains A antigens on red cells and B antibodies in plasma. Most common in Europe (about 40% of population). Associated with higher cortisol levels and better adaptation to agricultural diets.
Type B
Contains B antigens and A antibodies. More common in Asia (up to 25% in some regions). Some studies suggest higher susceptibility to certain infections but better resistance to others like E. coli.
Type AB
Contains both A and B antigens but no antibodies. The rarest blood type (about 5% globally). AB+ is the universal recipient for plasma transfusions. Some research suggests higher risk for certain cancers but lower risk for others.
Type O
No A or B antigens but both antibodies. Most common worldwide (about 45%). O- is the universal donor for red blood cells. Associated with lower risk of heart disease but higher risk for ulcers and fertility issues.

Blood Type Compatibility

Universal Donor:

O- can donate red blood cells to all blood types

Universal Recipient:

AB+ can receive red blood cells from all blood types

Plasma Donation:

AB is the universal plasma donor, while O can only receive from other O types

Global Distribution

Blood type distribution varies significantly across different populations and ethnic groups, reflecting human migration patterns and evolutionary adaptations to different environments and disease pressures.
